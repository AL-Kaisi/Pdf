**English Version**

**Key Concepts:**

  • Prodrugs
  • Intestinal tract metabolism
  • Digestive enzymes
  • Bioavailability
  • Parenteral dosage forms

**Definitions:**

  • **Prodrug:** A compound that is inactive in its native form but is easily metabolized to the active agent.
  • **Bioavailability:** The extent and rate at which a drug becomes available at its site of action.

**Important Information:**

  • Olsalazine is an example of a prodrug that is cleaved by intestinal bacteria into two equivalents of mesalamine.
  • Chloramphenicol palmitate is another example of a prodrug that is hydrolyzed in the intestinal tract to release the active antibiotic chloramphenicol and palmitic acid.
  • Prodrugs can be metabolized to smaller compounds or become metabolic precursors to the active form.
  • The gastrointestinal tract contains a large number of digestive and bacterial enzymes that can degrade drug molecules, making it challenging for new drugs to survive.
  • If a drug cannot survive in the intestinal tract or has low oral bioavailability, it may only be administered via parenteral dosage forms.

**Technical Terms:**

  • **Esterases:** Enzymes that break down ester linkages.
  • **Oral bioavailability:** The extent and rate at which a drug becomes available in the bloodstream after oral administration.
  • **Parenteral:** Relating to administration of drugs by injection or infusion.
