**Drug Design Strategies**

**Key Concepts:**

  • **Covalent Bonding**: The formation of a chemical bond between a drug molecule and a receptor, which can lead to prolonged effects or require cellular destruction for removal.
  • **Pharmacological Agents**: Substances that interact with receptors or enzymes to produce therapeutic effects.
  • **Receptor Binding**: The interaction between a drug molecule and its target receptor, which determines the drug's efficacy and duration of action.

**Important Information:**

  • The ideal drug design strategy involves creating molecules that bind favorably to their target receptors while dissociating quickly from the receptor site to be excreted.
  • Oral administration poses challenges, such as dissolution in the gastrointestinal tract, acid-base chemistry, and particle size variation affecting bioavailability.
  • Chemical modification can facilitate drug delivery, as seen with olsalazine, which bypasses first-pass metabolism by forming a dimer.
  • Modern drug design incorporates computational chemical techniques, knowledge of disease mechanisms, and receptor properties to predict optimal pharmacological activity.

**English Version**

**Key Concepts:**

  • Prodrugs
  • Intestinal tract metabolism
  • Digestive enzymes
  • Bioavailability
  • Parenteral dosage forms

**Definitions:**

  • **Prodrug:** A compound that is inactive in its native form but is easily metabolized to the active agent.
  • **Bioavailability:** The extent and rate at which a drug becomes available at its site of action.

**Important Information:**

  • Olsalazine is an example of a prodrug that is cleaved by intestinal bacteria into two equivalents of mesalamine.
  • Chloramphenicol palmitate is another example of a prodrug that is hydrolyzed in the intestinal tract to release the active antibiotic chloramphenicol and palmitic acid.
  • Prodrugs can be metabolized to smaller compounds or become metabolic precursors to the active form.
  • The gastrointestinal tract contains a large number of digestive and bacterial enzymes that can degrade drug molecules, making it challenging for new drugs to survive.
  • If a drug cannot survive in the intestinal tract or has low oral bioavailability, it may only be administered via parenteral dosage forms.

**Technical Terms:**

  • **Esterases:** Enzymes that break down ester linkages.
  • **Oral bioavailability:** The extent and rate at which a drug becomes available in the bloodstream after oral administration.
  • **Parenteral:** Relating to administration of drugs by injection or infusion.
